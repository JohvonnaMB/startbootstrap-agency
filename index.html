Obstacle Avoidance Robot

Built an obstacle avoiding robot on Arduino platform that uses an oscillating ultrasonic ranging module to navigate through a room while avoiding contact with all objects.

An Arduino motor shield was used to control 4 DC motors and 1 mini servo motor. The DC motors are used to actuate the wheels of the robot. The servo motor is used to rotate the ultrasonic ranging module as the robot drives around a room. A 3D printed part was designed in Solidworks to fasten the ultrasonic ranging module to the servo motor as it oscillates between 0 to 180 degrees.

An algorithm was design and implemented such that the robot drives in the direction of the furthest detected distance to an obstacle.

  • Date: April 2017

IoT Goosebot

Have you ever seen one of those videos of a box that simply turns itself off after being turned on by a user and thought, "...How useless?" Well I have found a way to make that box just a little less... useless! The University of Waterloo's 2016 1st Place EngHack Winner, was awarded to my two teammates and I, who designed a Waterloo themed useless box that uses an Android app to incorporate user customization. This not only added awesome IoT functionality to the box, but additionally gives the user the power to remotely define new patterns in which the box can turn itself off - via an array of light bulbs, speakers, and servo motors.

My team members and I started this project by building a wooden box at the machine shop that enclosed all the necessary hardware components. Next we wired, soldered, and assembled all of our circuit elements with an Arduino Uno driving the system. Finally, we developed the control program of the GooseBot that was written in C++ in such a way that allows the user's box to open in one of two ways: with preset patterns or patterns designed by the user on the Android app. Check out the code here!

Linear Caliper

I developed a caliper with 3 classmates that has the ability to measure lengths between 0 mm to 120 mm with a calculated maximum uncertainty of 11 mm. The caliper uses a rack and pinion to convert linear displacment into rotary motion, a potentiometer to convert the angular displacment of a gear into units of voltage, and a LCD to display the measurements to the user.

I lead the design of the rack and pinion that was laser cut from a sheet of 5mm thick transparent plastic. I designed the rack and pinion in AutoCAD to have an appropriate mesh when actuated.

  • Date: April 2016
  • Course: Experimental Measurement and Statistical Analysis

Humanoid Grasp Robot

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet consectetur.

The Hand Grasp Robot was created with the goal of building a mechatronic system that simulates the grasping action of the human hand around everyday objects. In order to do so, the robot was designed to simulate power and precision gripping; the two most common grasps the human hand uses to hold or suspend objects. The robotic hand can help people who struggle with disabilities that impede their ability to grasp by reducing the hindrance of performing daily tasks that require these two grasping skills.

The robot was made with a LEGO NXT Platform and programmed in RobotC language. It was created with three appendages that modeled two fingers and a thumb. Each of these were built with rotating joints and string to represent tendons and joints of the human hand, allowing the robot to move in a human like manor. The final robot was able to suspend a full water bottle with a power grasp and a pencil with a precision grasp.

  • Date: December 2014
  • Course: Mechatronics Engineering

SMT PCB Sensor Design

Designed a robot that uses an embedded system to leverage a surface-mount PCB designed in Altium and a 8-bit microcontroller (PIC16F873A-I/SO). Wrote the algorithm in C, instructing the robot to navigate through a course by following a black line, while concurrently detecting changes in temperature and magnetic field in its surrounding environment. All sensors on the robot were custom designed and implemented as follows:

• Light Tracking Sensor - using infared LEDS and photodiodes

• Temperature Sensor - using a thermistor

• Magnet Detecting Sensor - using hall effect sensor

• Current Sensor - to assess battery power

• Encoders - using infared LEDS, phototransistors, and encoder plate

  • Date: December 2016
  • Course: Sensors and Instrumentation

2-DOF End Effector

As a dedicated member of the University of Waterloo’s Mars Rover Team, I designed a mechanical system used as the wrist of a rover we designed to compete in the 2017 University Rover Challenge. The wrist is comprised of a 3 bevel gear system that allows the end effector to rotate with 2 degrees of rotational freedom.

The entire system was modelled as a Solidworks assembly. Once validated, the system was machined and implemented into the rover.
